Foundations & Definitions

Lessons 1–2
Two types of F&B establishment
Institutional — supports people, not profit-driven, usually subsidized/outsourced, cost-oriented (school, hospital, prison). Very concentrated market.
Commercial — operates for profit, market-oriented (restaurants, bars). Limited-service = concentrated; full-service = fragmented/concentrated.
Fragmented vs Concentrated market
Fragmented — no single firm has enough share to steer the industry. Concentrated — a few large firms dominate and shape direction.
Product / Restaurant life cycle
1 Development (cost-heavy, no revenue) → 2 Introduction (slow sales, costly marketing) → 3 Growth (sales & profit rise) → 4 Maturity (stable sales, lower cost, max profit — make it last) → 5 Decline (poorly-managed maturity). Nokia = failed to manage maturity.
Viability factors (PESTEL)
External (can't change): PESTEL — Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal — plus competition, suppliers, customers. Internal (can change): operations, staffing, quality, family. Failure driven more by internal factors.
Profit disruptors
Internal: labour cost, food waste, coulage (theft of product), employee turnover, wait times, price gouging, portion control. External: food trends, Instagram ("camera eats first"), uniqueness, ease of modernization.
Where the money goes (typical)
Food 33% · Labour 36% · Rent 9% · Direct OpEx 11% · Indirect OpEx 6% · Profit ≈ 5%. Main cost sources = Food, Beverage, Labour. Beverage costs are grouped with food costs.
3 key financial reports
Balance Sheet (A = L + SE) · Statement of Cash Flows · P&L / Income Statement (revenue − expenses over a period). P&L = Income − Expenses = Total Sales − Expenses.
P&L structure (order)
1 Sales/Revenue (Food + Beverage + Merchandise) → 2 Prime Costs (Total Labour + Total Cost of Sales) → 3 Controllable Expenses (marketing, repairs) → 4 Non-Controllable (rent, depreciation). Standardized by USALI. Recorded net of VAT.
Variable / Fixed / Mixed costs
Variable — moves with output (COGS, supplies, repairs). Fixed — unchanged by volume (rent, leasing, depreciation, subscriptions). Mixed — both (marketing, labour w/ overtime, music).
Cost behaviour as sales rise
Fixed: % of sales ↓, total $ same. Variable: % of sales same, total $ ↑. Mixed: % ↓, total $ ↑.
Direct vs Indirect costs
Direct — traceable to a specific product/unit (direct labour, materials). Indirect — apply to more than one activity, can't assign to one object.
Controllable vs Non-controllable
Controllable — manager can raise/lower (marketing, repairs). Non-controllable — manager can't (rent, depreciation). Manager focuses on controllable; owner on all.
Vertical vs Horizontal analysis
Vertical — express each line as % of Total Sales ("where the money goes" in one period). Horizontal — compare same line across periods/budget via variance (spot trends).

P&L & Profit Formulas

profit waterfall
Gross Profit
Gross Profit = Total Sales − Total Cost of Sales
Actual Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS
Per item: GP $ = Sale Price $ − Food Cost $  ·  GP % = Sale Price % − Food Cost %.
Total Gross Profit
Total GP = Total Sales − Total Cost of Sales
Total Item GP = GP per item × number sold
Prime Cost
Prime Cost = Total Cost of Sales + Total Labour
The two biggest, most vital cost blocks. Profit on Prime Cost = Total Sales − Prime Costs.
Prime Cost Ratio %
Prime Cost % = (Labour + Cost of Sales) ÷ Total Revenue
High prime cost = bad. ~78% is too high to open a restaurant financially.
Controllable Income
Controllable Income = Total Sales − Prime Cost − Total Expenses
Operating Income (EBIT)
Operating Income = Controllable Income − Total Non-Controllable Expenses
Income Before Taxes
Income Before Taxes = Operating Income − Interest Expense
Net Income
Net Income = Income Before Taxes − Income Tax
Before-Tax Profit
Before-Tax Profit = After-Tax Profit ÷ (1 − Tax Rate)
Variant seen in notes: After-Tax Profit ÷ CM share (used inside after-tax BEP).
Total Revenue (two views)
Total Revenue = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs + Profit
Total Revenue = Days Open × Guests/Day × Guest Check
Total Cost of Sales
Total Cost of Sales = Food + Beverage + Merchandise (cost of each)
Note: your notes write "food sales + beverage sales + merchandise" — read it as the cost of each category, not sales.
Total Expenses / Total Labour
Total Expenses = Total Operational Expenses + Total Controllable Costs
Total Labour = Management + Staff + Employee Benefits
Gross Profit by category
Food GP = Food Sales − Food Costs
Beverage GP = Beverage Sales − Beverage Costs
Merchandise GP = Merch Sales − Merch Costs

Cost-Percentage Formulas

type % to find these fast
Food Cost %
Food Cost % = COGS ÷ Food Revenue × 100
Food Cost % = (Total Cost ÷ Total Sales) × 100
Food Cost % = Sale Price % − Gross Profit %
COGS = food cost. Today: cost today ÷ sales today. To-date: cost to-date ÷ sales to-date.
Cost of Sales %
Cost of Sales % = Cost of Sales ÷ Revenue
Beverage Cost %
Beverage Cost % = Cost of Beverage ÷ Beverage Sales
Labour Cost %
Labour Cost % = Cost of Labour ÷ Total Revenue
Measures labour efficiency.
Minimum Operating Cost %
Min Operating Cost % = Food Cost % + Other Variable Costs %
Variable Cost % (from CM)
Variable Cost % = 1 − Contribution Margin %
Any line item as % (Vertical)
% of X = Value of X ÷ Total Sales
Exception: Food/Beverage/Merchandise Cost % use their own category sales as the denominator, not total sales.

Contribution Margin

the engine of BEP
Contribution Margin ($)
CM = Sales − Variable Costs
CM = Total Sales − Total Variable Costs
What's left to cover fixed costs + profit after variable costs.
CM per Cover
CM/cover = Sales per Cover − Variable Costs per Cover
Drives BEP in covers. Sales per cover = Sales ÷ no. covers; VC per cover = VC ÷ no. covers.
CM % (margin ratio / MVCR)
CM % = CM ÷ Total Sales
CM Ratio = (Revenue − Variable Costs) ÷ Revenue
Also called Marginal Variable Contribution Rate. CM % = 100% − Variable Cost %.
CM Share (for BEP $)
CM Share = (Total Sales − Total Variable Costs) ÷ Total Sales
Denominator for BEP in sales $.
CM % from fixed costs + profit
CM % = (Fixed Costs + Before-Tax Profit) ÷ Total Revenue
Rearrangement: you can swap (Sales − Variable Costs) with (Fixed Costs + Profit).
CM $ from average check
CM $ = Average Guest Check × CM %

Break-Even Point (BEP)

revenue = total cost
BEP — definition
BEP: Revenue = Total Costs (Fixed + Variable) → zero profit
Minimum sales needed to cover all costs. Always ROUND UP covers (budget & BEP).
BEP in Covers
BEP (covers) = Fixed Costs ÷ CM per Cover
CM per cover = Sales/cover − Variable Cost/cover.
BEP in Sales ($)
BEP (sales) = Fixed Costs ÷ CM Share
BEP with Desired Profit
BEP (sales) = (Fixed Costs + Desired Profit) ÷ CM Share
BEP with After-Tax Profit
BEP (sales) = (Fixed Costs + Before-Tax Profit) ÷ CM Share
BEP covers (after-tax) = BEP sales (after-tax) ÷ Sales per Cover
First convert after-tax → before-tax: Before-Tax = After-Tax ÷ (1 − tax rate).
BEP in Days
BEP (days) = BEP $ ÷ Average Daily Sales
Average Daily Sales = Monthly Total Sales ÷ Days Open.
BEP in Guests
BEP (guests) = BEP $ ÷ Average Check
BEP of production
BEP (production) = BEP $ ÷ Selling Price
CM per unit = (Selling Price − Variable Cost) ÷ unit.
Worked example — BEP in covers
Sales 60,000 · Fixed 15,000 · Variable 35,000 · Covers 200 Sales/cover = 60,000 ÷ 200 = 300 VC/cover = 35,000 ÷ 200 = 175 CM/cover = 300 − 175 = 125 BEP covers = 15,000 ÷ 125 = 120 covers to break even
Procedure — covers to hit a profit target
Step 1 CM % (MVCR) = 100% − Variable Cost % Step 2 BEP for desired profit = (Fixed Cost + Profit) ÷ CM % Step 3 Covers = BEP $ ÷ Average Check (always round up)
Procedure — sales & covers at BEP from average check
Step 1 CM % = ((Average Check − Variable Costs) ÷ Average Check) × 100 Step 2 BEP $ = Fixed Costs ÷ CM%(as decimal) Step 3 Covers = BEP $ ÷ Average Check
Minimum Sales Point
MSP = Minimum Labour Cost ÷ (1 − Minimum Operating Cost %)
Revenue needed to justify staying open longer. Min Operating Cost % = Food Cost % + other variable costs. Min Labour Cost = rate/hour × hours.

Revenue & Seating KPIs

RevPASH · turnover · occupancy
RevPASH
RevPASH = Total Revenue ÷ (No. Seats × Hours Open)
RevPASH = Occupancy % × Average Check
Revenue per Available Seat Hour — how hard each seat works per hour.
RevPAS
RevPAS = Revenue ÷ Number of Seats Available
RevPSM (per m²)
RevPSM = Total Sales ÷ Total m²
Revenue per Cover
Revenue/Cover = Total Sales ÷ Total Covers
Food/Cover = Food Sales ÷ Covers · Bev/Cover = Bev Sales ÷ Covers
Average Check
Average Check = Revenue ÷ Number of Covers
Average Check (per dish) = Revenue ÷ Quantity Sold
Average Daily Sales
Avg Daily Sales = Monthly Total Sales ÷ Days Open
Seat Turnover
Seat Turnover = No. Covers ÷ Number of Seats
= Seat utilisation. Seat = empty; cover = occupied. (40 ÷ 80 = 0.5)
Average Daily Seat Turnover
= (Breakfast + Lunch + Dinner Covers) ÷ (No. Seats × 3)
Use when given individual meal-period turnovers.
Ideal Seat Turnover
Ideal Seat Turnover = Minutes Open ÷ Average Service Cycle Time
Ideal Seat Turnover Rate = Real Seat Turnover ÷ Occupancy Rate
Service Cycle Time
SCT = Σ(table usage time, similar tables) ÷ Number of similar tables
Maximum Capacity
Max Capacity = (No. Seats × Minutes Open) ÷ Avg Service Cycle Time
Occupancy Ratio / %
Occupancy Ratio = Actual Turnover ÷ Ideal Seat Turnover
Occupancy % = (No. Covers ÷ Max Capacity) × 100
Occupancy % = (RevPASH ÷ Average Check) × 100
Worked example — RevPASH
100 seats, 7 days. Lunch 12–15 (3h), Dinner 17–22 (5h) Lunch = 5,000 ÷ (100 × 3) = 16.67 Dinner = 15,000 ÷ (100 × 5) = 30.00

Guest / Attractiveness KPIs

capture · return · walk-in
Capture Rate
Capture Rate = In-House Guests per Meal Period ÷ Total Hotel Guests
Return Guest %
Return Guest % = Total Return Guests ÷ Total Guests Served
Walk-in %
Walk-in % = Total Walk-ins ÷ Total Guests Served
Internal / External hotel KPIs

Menu Engineering & Popularity

stars · plowhorses · puzzles · dogs
Item Gross Profit
Item GP = Menu Price − Food Cost
Total Item GP = GP × Number Sold
Average Gross Profit / Profitability
Avg GP = Σ Total GP ÷ Total Number Sold
Avg Menu Profitability = Σ Total GP ÷ Total Dishes Sold
The profitability cut-line: above = Star/Puzzle, below = Plowhorse/Dog.
Popularity %
Popularity % = Number Sold ÷ Total Number Sold
Average Popularity (cut-line)
Avg Popularity = (100% ÷ Number of Menu Items) × 70%
e.g. 4 items → (100/4) × 70% = 17.5%. Above = popular (Star/Plowhorse), below = unpopular (Dog/Puzzle).
Menu Engineering quadrants — what to do
High PopularityLow Popularity
High ProfitSTAR — don't touch it, do nothing.PUZZLE — rearrange menu, promote, or lower price.
Low ProfitPLOWHORSE — rework recipe, portion control, raise price.DOG — rebrand, redesign, or remove.
Compare each item to Average Popularity (popularity axis) and Average Gross Profit (profit axis). Limitations: time-consuming, ignores competition (internal view only).
Potential Food Cost
Item's Potential FC = Number Sold × Cost per Dish
Menu's Potential FC = Σ each item's potential FC
Potential Food Cost %
Item PFC % = (Item Potential FC ÷ Revenue per Item) × 100
Menu PFC % = (Menu Potential FC ÷ Food Revenue) × 100
Compare to actual FC% — gap = waste, theft, over-portioning, etc.
Total food cost / menu revenue
Total Food Costs = Units Sold × Food Cost
Total Menu Revenue = Σ Total Item Revenue
Average Menu Price
Avg Menu Price = Σ Prices ÷ Number of Dishes
Different from Average Check (= revenue ÷ number sold). Used in OMNES & category ratio.

Pricing Methods & Strategies

factor · gross margin · psychology
Pricing Factor (Factor Method)
Pricing Factor = 100% ÷ Desired Food Cost %
20%→5 · 25%→4 · 33.33%→3. Most common method; ignores value.
Selling Price (factor)
Selling Price = Food Cost of Dish × Pricing Factor
Sale Price = Food Cost $ ÷ Food Cost %
Ideal Selling Price (cost-based)
Ideal Selling Price = Cost ÷ Desired Food Cost %
e.g. 6.01 ÷ 0.34 ≈ 17.67. Then value-based pricing can charge more (e.g. 25).
Cost % Method
Selling Price = Cost of Product ÷ Desired Product Cost %
Selling price of a specific item
Item Selling Price = Cost of Item ÷ Food Cost % of that item
Procedure — menu item selling price (markup)
Step 1 Mark-up factor = 100 ÷ Desired Food Cost % Step 2 Selling Price (VAT excl) = Cost × Mark-up factor
Gross Margin (Avg GM Method)
Gross Margin = Non-Food Costs + Desired Net Profit
Considers operating + fixed costs. Used by large manufacturers (McDonald's). The only method taking fixed costs into direct account.
Procedure — Average Gross Margin Method
Step 1 Gross Profit = Non-Food Costs + Desired Net Profit Step 2 Unit Gross Profit = Gross Profit ÷ Expected Customers Step 3 Selling Price = Unit Gross Margin + Cost of Dish
Procedure — Gross Margin per unit / dish
Step 1 Gross Margin % = 100% − Food Cost % Step 2 Gross Margin $ = Revenue × GM% Step 3 Unit GM = GM $ ÷ Number of dishes sold GM per customer = Gross Margin ÷ Expected Customers
Gross Profit Margin %
GP Margin = (Menu Price − Raw Cost) ÷ Menu Price
Cost-based vs Value-based
Cost-based — price from cost + margin; customer ignored. Value-based — price from what the customer will pay (location, chef, experience). Higher margin on cheap items, lower on costly ones.
Positioning strategies
Skimming — max profit/ROI. Alignment — match competition, hold share. Penetration — grow share, market disruption (new product). Survival — cover at least variable costs. Plus Premium, Bundle, Loss-Leader, Multiple, Couponing.
Psychological pricing
Charm 9.95 vs 10 · Decoy makes others look good · Cent-less £10 > £10.00 · Remove $ sign · Innumeracy "buy 1 get 1 free" · Low-syllable 29 feels lighter than 27.70 · Differential (sales).
Price blending & dynamic pricing
Price blending — high- & low-cost items offset; margins brought closer; goal = revenue. Dynamic — raise prices on internal/external factors & availability.

OMNES Principle

4 principles of a balanced menu
Principle 1 — Price Range
Variance Ratio = Highest Priced Item ÷ Lowest Priced Item
Ideal 2.5–3. Good: 25/10 = 2.5. Bad: 60/10 = 6 or 15/10 = 1.5.
Principle 2 — Price Spread
Spread = (Highest − Lowest) ÷ 3
Split menu into 3 bands; ideal distribution 25% – 50% – 25% (low–mid–high).
Principle 3 — Customer Reaction
Category Ratio = Average Check ÷ Average Menu Price
Ideal 0.9–0.99. <0.9 = too expensive · >1.00 = too cheap.
Principle 4 — Promotion
Promote items in the middle (median) range of the price spread.
Worked example — OMNES (avg check $20, promotes Quinoa Salad $16)
P1 25 ÷ 10 = 2.5 → in range ✔ MET P2 (25 − 10) ÷ 3 = 5 bands; 3 low / 2 mid / 2 high → not 25-50-25 ✘ NOT met P3 20 ÷ 16.57 = 1.2 → not 0.9-0.99 ✘ NOT met P4 Quinoa $16 = median item, being promoted ✔ MET

Yield & Butcher Tests · Recipe Costing

AP · EP · waste · conversion
Yield vs Butcher test
Yield test — non-"alive" products (vegetables). Butcher test — "alive" products (meat, fish), looks at bones & scraps, spoken in portions. Same formulas.
AP / EP / Waste relationships
EP Weight = AP Weight − Waste
AP Weight = EP + Waste
Waste = AP Weight − EP Weight
EP = AP − bones − scraps. AP = gross weight purchased; EP = usable net weight.
Yield Percentage
Yield % = (EP ÷ AP) × 100
How much to buy (AP Weight)
AP Weight = EP Weight ÷ Yield %
Edible Portion Price (real cost)
EP Price = AP Price ÷ Yield %
Value per kg (EP) = AP Price ÷ Yield %
Conversion Factor (Factor Method)
Conversion Factor = Desired Yield ÷ Current Yield
Percentage Method
% of Total = Ingredient Weight ÷ Total Recipe Weight
New Recipe Amount = % of Total × Total Amount Required
Recipe / Portion cost
Recipe Cost per Ingredient = EP $ per Unit × Quantity
Cost per Portion = Recipe Cost ÷ Number of Portions
AP $/Unit × Yield % = Recipe Cost (per dish)
Portion pieces & cost
Portion Pieces = Weight ÷ Portion Size
Portion Cost per Piece = Total Value ÷ Number of Pieces
Total Value = Weight (kg) × Value per kg
Z-Cost
Free items given to guests (bread, oil, butter, salt, pepper). Added into food cost at Step 3.
Standardized recipe (6 steps, Step 1)
Contains: item name, total yield, portion size, ingredient list, method, time & temp, special instructions. Pros: consistency, accurate purchasing/costing/pricing, easier training. Always standardize.

Purchasing & Receiving

documents · methods · ABC
Purchasing vs Procurement
Purchasing — the act of buying goods/services; run by Finance (control + document handling). Procurement — finding, agreeing terms, tendering; managed by everyone (heads of dept) building supplier relationships.
3 Purchasing questions
1) Make or buy? compare cost/labour/storage. 2) How many vendors? ~3–4 (avoid overcharging & supply risk). 3) Centralized vs Decentralized? — best is both. Centralized = control/pricing but bureaucracy; decentralized = fast/specific but disorganized.
Purchasing document flow (order)
1 Spec Sheet (intended use most important; no price) → 2 Purchase Request / Requisition (internal, new items) → 3 Bid Sheet (compare suppliers; negotiate) → 4 Purchase Order (PO) — first official legal doc, buyer→seller.
Receiving documents
Delivery Note arrives with goods → Invoice (bill of sale, seller→buyer, confirms deal) → Credit Note (seller→buyer, credits account after a mistake: overstated invoice, wrong discount, spoilt/broken goods, requirements not met).
Receiving process
PO → delivery + delivery note → check items/quality/quantity match → store with FIFO. Ideally received by the orderer/purchaser. If anything is wrong, DO NOT sign.
Purchasing approach combinations
Fixed DateVariable Date
Fixed QuantityAutomatic Purchasing (same amount, same time)Periodic Reorder Level (order when stock hits reorder point)
Variable QuantityMethod of Replenishment (fixed date, varying amount; check stock)Opportunist Purchasing (mostly food)
ABC Classification
A: 20% of items = 70% of consumption value (high value — alcohol, caviar; tight control). B: 30% of items = 25% value (moderate control). C: 50% of items = 5% value (salt, straws; minimal control). Consumption Value = Usage × Cost.
Supply Chain dimensions
Physical — how product moves. Informational — what/where/how/how much/when. Financial — how money + bills move.

Inventory & Valuation

FIFO · LIFO · WAP · turnover
Annual Consumption Value
ACV = Amount Used × Cost per Unit
ACV % = Individual ACV ÷ Total ACV
Compute ACV% then compare to ABC bands to classify A/B/C.
Average Monthly Inventory
Avg Monthly Inventory = (Initial + Final Inventory) ÷ 2
Rate of Inventory Turnover
Inventory Turnover = Cost of Sales ÷ Average Monthly Inventory
Average Age of Inventory
Avg Age = Days in Month ÷ Rate of Inventory Turnover
Inventory ratios
Inventory to Cost of Sales = Avg Monthly Inventory ÷ Cost of Sales
Sales to Inventory = Revenue ÷ Avg Monthly Inventory
Storeroom book value
Closing Book Value = Opening Stock + Purchases − Issues
Storeroom Inv of Day = Prev Day + Purchases − Issues
Bin card per item; check ≥ once a month on the last day.
Inventory valuation methods
FIFO — issues expensed at oldest price; stock valued at newest. LIFO — issues at newest price; stock valued at oldest. WAP (Weighted Average Price) — value of units available ÷ number available; best compromise with frequent price swings, takes history into account, most accurate but harder. Last Purchased Price — value at most recent price (closest date before issue date); easy but less accurate.
Counting: Perpetual = continuous (subtract after each use). Physical = counted (usually last day of month) — most accurate.

Actual Food Cost

consumed vs sold · daily food cost
COGS / Total Available
COGS = Total Available − Closing Inventory
Total Available = Opening Inventory + Purchases
Food Cost
Food Cost = Opening Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory
Food Cost $ = Direct Purchases + Issued
Cost of Food Consumed
Cost of Food Consumed = Food Cost − Transfer Out + Transfer In
All food consumed by guests + employees. This figure goes into the P&L.
Cost of Food Sold
Cost of Food Sold = Cost of Food Consumed − (Employee Meals + Free Giveaways)
Cost of food used to earn revenue (strips non-revenue items: inter-outlet/unit transfers + non-revenue food).
Daily running cost
Total Cost Today = Direct Purchase + Issues + (corr. bev→food) − (corr. food→bev)
Total Cost to Date = Prev. Day To-Date + Total Cost Today
Daily Food Cost build-up
+ Cost of Direct Purchases (straight to kitchen, e.g. bread)
+ Cost of Store Issues (from main storage via requisition)
± Inter-Kitchen Transfers · ± Inter-Unit Transfers (food↔beverage)
= Food Available for Sale
− Adjustments (between depts / complimentary) − Cost of Employee Meals
= Daily Food Cost
The 6 Food Cost steps
1 Write recipes & sub-recipes (yield, portions) · 2 Yield & Butcher tests (AP vs EP) · 3 Calculate the cost (incl. Z-cost) · 4 Sales statistics (incl. comps, re-fires, re-cooks — always post) · 5 Potential Food Cost · 6 Potential vs Actual FC. Potential FC is lower; Actual FC is higher (waste, theft, over-portioning, poor receiving, unrecorded sales).
Where mis-posted food goes
Employee meals → Labour (employee benefits). Gratis to bar customers → Bar. Promotional (happy hour, coupons) → Marketing. Nothing extra should sit in Food Cost.

Beverage Cost

cost · sales value · quantity
3 Beverage calculation methods
Cost Method — inventory-based actual cost (Cost of Beverage ÷ Beverage Sales). Sales Value Method — potential vs actual bottle revenue. Quantity Method — mL that should have poured vs actual.
Beverage cost & GP
Beverage Cost % = Cost of Beverage ÷ Beverage Sales
Beverage GP = Beverage Sales − Beverage Costs
Bev Revenue per Cover = Beverage Sales ÷ Total Covers
Cost / drinks per bottle
Cost per Drink = Bottle Price ÷ Drinks per Bottle
Usable volume = bottle − spillage allowance. Recipe must include mixers (tonic, ice, lime), not just the spirit.
Worked example — Sales Value Method (gin)
1L gin, 40 mL pour, 2% spillage → usable 980 mL Drinks = 980 ÷ 40 = 24.5 pours Potential = 24.5 × $12 = $294 Actual POS revenue = $252 Missing = $294 − $252 = $42 → $42 ÷ $12 = 3.5 drinks unaccounted
High cost causes & controls
Causes: over/under-pouring, drinks not entered in POS, giveaways, employees drinking, theft, serving cheap as premium. Controls: standard glassware/measures/recipes, mark bottles, mystery shopper, bottle-for-bottle (1 empty = 1 new).
Beverage inventory rules
Monthly, two people (finance + bar). Beer bottles counted individually; kegs by weight; everything counted/measured. Visual count: ~25% left ≈ 0.2.

Labour Cost & Productivity

turnover · FTE · efficiency
Labour Cost % (efficiency)
Labour Cost % = Cost of Labour ÷ Total Revenue
Labour per cover / hour
Labour Cost per Cover = Labour Cost ÷ Covers Served
Covers per Labour Cost = Covers Served ÷ Labour Cost
Covers per Labour Hour = Covers Served ÷ Labour Hours
Sales per Labour Hour
Sales per Labour Hour = Total Sales ÷ Labour Hours
Productivity Rate
Productivity Rate = Output ÷ Input
Output ↑ / input same → PR ↑. Input ↑ / output same → PR ↓.
Employee Turnover Rate
Turnover Rate = (No. Staff Who Left ÷ Average No. Staff) × 100
Average No. Staff = (Number at Start + Number at End) ÷ 2
Note: your doc writes "(start − end) ÷ 2" — the standard correct form is (start + end) ÷ 2. US restaurant avg ≈ 74.9%.
Labour budgeting
Labour Cost Increase = Labour Cost × New Budgeted Increase
Minimum Labour Cost = Rate per Hour × Hours
Number of employees to hire (FTE)
Employees to Hire = (Open Days ÷ Days Worked Ratio) × Employees Wanted per Day
Ratio = Number of days open ÷ days worked by one employee.
Payroll definitions
Payroll = gross pay for work. Labour Expense = wages + housing/uniform/transport. Fixed payroll (salaries, little control) vs Variable payroll (hourly, ~100% control). Payroll = controllable; taxes/insurance = uncontrollable.

Variance, Flow-Through & Forecasting

budgeting procedures
Variance
Variance $ = New − Old
Variance % = (New − Old) ÷ Old
Flow-Through (Flow-Thru)
Flow-Thru = (Variance GOP ÷ Variance Sales) × 100
= (Current OP − Prev OP) ÷ (Current Rev − Prev Rev)
Negative flow-thru (negative GOP variance) = revenue grew but costs poorly managed — more money in, but more cost too.
Budget vs Forecast
Budget sets expectations (year plan, from history or scratch). Forecast updates them based on reality — the bridge between budget and actuals. P&L reports the actual outcome.
Budgeted change in covers
Number of Covers = Actual Covers × Budgeted Increase
Always ROUND UP, regardless of decimal. Same for increase/decrease in covers.
Budgeting food/labour
Food sales & costs: average per cover → adjust price → × new budgeted covers. Labour: Labour Cost × budgeted increase.
Procedure — Best Revenue Forecast
Option A (added seats): 1 Actual Avg Check = Total Revenue ÷ No. Seats = x 2 New Potential = Added Seats × x = y 3 Best Revenue = Total Revenue + y Option B (% increase): 1 Added seats ≈ x% increase 2 Best Revenue = Total Revenue × (1 + increase %)
Procedure — Cost % Forecast
1 Actual food cost = Food Sales × Food Cost % = x 2 New cost (increased by y%) = x × (1 + y%) = z 3 New Food Cost % = (z ÷ Food Sales) × 100
Procedure — Average Check Variance %
1 New Average Check = Revenue ÷ Covers = x 2 Difference = New Avg Check − old average sale per guest 3 Variance % = Difference ÷ old (× 100)

Decision Rules — Quick Reference

"is it good or bad?"
Cost classification — 2 questions
Variable or Fixed? → "Do my costs increase when I produce more units?" Yes = Variable, No = Fixed (both = Mixed).
Direct or Indirect? → "Can I trace this cost back to one product/unit?" Yes = Direct, No = Indirect.
OMNES ideal thresholds
P1 Price Range ratio 2.5–3 · P2 Spread 25%-50%-25% · P3 Category Ratio 0.9–0.99 (<0.9 too pricey, >1 too cheap) · P4 promote middle items.
Menu engineering cut-lines
Popularity axis vs (100% ÷ items) × 70%; Profit axis vs Average Gross Profit. High-High = Star (keep) · Low-profit/High-pop = Plowhorse (rework/raise price) · High-profit/Low-pop = Puzzle (promote) · Low-Low = Dog (remove).
Pricing factor quick table
Desired FC 20% → factor 5 · 25% → 4 · 33.33% → 3.  (Factor = 100 ÷ desired FC%.)
Exam reflexes
Always round covers UP. Beverage cost grouped with food. Prime cost ~78% = too high, don't open. P&L recorded net of VAT. Potential FC < Actual FC. If receiving is wrong, don't sign.
